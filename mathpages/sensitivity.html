A \(\textbf{n-dimensional hypercube graph}\), denoted by \(\mathbf{Q}^n\), is a graph whose vertices are vectors in \(\{0,1\}^n\) and two vectors are adjacent if they differ in exactly one coordinate.

Given \(G=(V,E)\) a graph, and a subset of vertex set \(S \subset V\), then the \(\textbf{induced subgraph}\) \(G[S]\) is a graph whose vertex set is \(S\) and whose edge set is all possible edges in \(G\) whose end vertices are in \(S\).

For every interger \(n \geq 1 \), and \(H\) an arbitrary induced subgraph of \(\mathcal{Q}^n\) with \(2^{n-1}+1\) vertices, then \[\triangle(H) \geq \sqrt{n}\]. Remember that \( \triangle(H)\) means the maximum degree of any vertex in the graph \(H\) and the degree of a vertex is the number of edges connected to it.

For \(x \in \{0,1\}^n\), and \(S \subset [n]\), we denote \(x^S\) the binary vector obtained from \(x\) by flipping all indices in \(S\). For example, suppose \(n=3\), \(x=(1,0,1)\) and \(S=\{1,2\}\) then \(x^S = (0,1,1)\).

We now introduce sensitivity of a boolean function.
For a function \(f : \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\} \), the \(\textbf{local sensitivity}\) \(s(f,x)\) on the input \(x\) is defined as the number of indices \(i\) such that \(f(x) \neq f(x^{i})\). Then from the local sensitivity, we can get the \(\textbf{sensitivity}\) of the boolean function to be defined as :\[s(f)= \max_x s(f,x) \].
Sensitivity is then, is an indication of how many varaibles are the determining factors that will change the result of a bolean function. It is as if the other variable are not very important towards the evaluation of the boolean function. Given boolean function \(f : \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\} \), the \(\textbf{local block sensitivity}\) \(bs(f,x)\) is the maximum number of disjoint subsets \(B_1,...,B_k\) of \([n]\) such that for each \(B_i\), \(f(x) \neq f(x^{B_i})\). The \(\textbf{block sensitivity}\) \(bs(f) = \max_x bs(f,x)\) The subsets \(B_i\) are refered to as blocks

We can make element in each block be singleton element and then this means that the block sensitivity is at least as large as sensitivity for a boolean function: \(bs(f) \geq s(f)\). So the sensitivity conjecture is that these two quantities are polynomially related:
\(\textbf{Sensitivity Conjecture}\): There exists an absolute constant \(C >0 \) such that for any boolean function \(f\), \(bs(f) \leq s(f)^C \) So the sensitivity conjecture can be proved in the manner described below, in particular, if we could show that the main result of the paper, we could use that result to fill the gap in the below arguments toward the proof of the sensitivity conjecture.
The following are equivalent for any monotone function \(h : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{R} \):
  1. For any induced subgraph \(H\) of \(Q^n\) where \(|V(H)| \neq 2^{n-1} \), we have \(\Gamma(H) \geq h(n)\)
  2. For any boolean function \(f\), we have \(s(f) \geq h(deg(f)) \)
We associate to the subgraph \(G\), a boolean function \(g\) such that \(g(x) =1 \iff x \in V(G) \). Now notice that \(s(g,x)\) counts the number of neighbhors of \(x\) in \(Q^n\) that is not adjacent to \(x\) in \(G\). Therefore, \(deg_G(x) = n - s(g,x) \). By the same reasoning, we have for \(x \in Q^n-G \Rightarrow deg_{Q^n-G}(x) = n - s(g,x)\). Let's denote by \(E(g)\), the average value of \(g\). Ok so now we are ready to proof the main result of the paper, we first state the Cauchy interlacing theorem that we'll use:
Let \(A\) be a symmetric \(n \times n\) matrix, and \(B\) be a \(m \times m \) principal submatrix \(A\) consiting of the first \(m\) rows and \(m\) columns of \(A\) for some \(m < n \). If \(\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq ...\lambda_n\) are the eigenvalues of \(A\) and \( \mu_1 \geq \mu_2 \geq ... \mu_m\) are the eigenvalues of \(B\) then: \[\forall 1 \leq i \leq m, \lambda_i \geq \mu_i \geq \lambda_{i+n-m} \]
We have another presentation dedicated to this theorem. Now, let's look at the following sequence of matrices:
We define a sequence of symmetric square matrices iteratively as follow: \[ A_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1\\ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, A_n = \begin{bmatrix} A_{n-1} & I \\ I & -A_{n-1} \end{bmatrix}\] Then \(A_n \) is a \(2^n \times 2^n \) matrix whose eigenvalues are \(\sqrt{n}\) of multiplicity \(2^{n-1}\), and \(-\sqrt{n}\) of multiplicity \(2^{n-1}\).
We first show that \(A_n^2 =nI\). we do so by induction on \(n\).
  • Base case, \(n=1\), then \(A_1^2=I\)
  • Inductive step: Suppose that the claim is true for \(n = \{1,...,k\}\), we now show that \(A_{k+1}^2 = (k+1)I\). By definition, \[A_{k+1}= \begin{bmatrix} A_k & I \\ I & -A_k \end{bmatrix} \] so by inductive hypothesis, we get: \[ A_{k+1}^2= \begin{bmatrix} A_k^2+I & 0 \\ 0 & A_k^2+I \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} kI+I & 0\\ 0 & kI+I \end{bmatrix} = (k+1)I\]
So if \(v\) is an eigenvector of \(A_n\) with eigenvalue \(\lambda\), then \(nv = nI v = A_n A_n v = \lambda A_n v= \lambda^2 v\). Therefore, eigenvalue of \(A_n\) must be \( \pm \sqrt{n}\). And since \(Tr(A_n) =0\) by construction of \(A_n\) and that the sum of eigenvalues equals the trace, it must be that each \(\pm \sqrt{n}\) happends half of times.
If we change all \(-1\) in \(A_n\) to \(1\) then the resulting matrix correspond to the adjacency matrix of the hypercube \(\mathcal{Q}^n\). This is seen by the fact that we make \(\mathcal{Q_n}\) from two copy of \(\mathcal{Q}^{n-1}\) by gluing their corresponding vertices.
Suppose \(H\) is an m-vertex undirected graph, and \(A\) is a symmetric matrix whose entries are in \(\{-1,0,1\}\) and whose rows and columns are indexed by \(V(H)\), and whenever \(u\) and \(v\) are non-adjacent in \(H\), \(A_{u,v}=0\).
let \(v\) be a eigenvector corresponding to \(\lambda_1\), then \(\lambda_1v =Av\). Whithout loss of generality, assume \(v_1\) is the coordinate of \(v\) that has the largest absolute value. Then \[ \lambda_1v_1 = |(Av)_1| = |\sum_{j=1}^m A_{1,j}v_j| \leq \sum_{j=1} |A_{1,j}v_j| \leq \triangle(H)|v_1| \] From this we conclude: \[\lambda_1 \leq \triangle(H)\]
